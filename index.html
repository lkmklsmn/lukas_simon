about

I'm an Assistant Professor and Group Leader of Computational Biology at the Therapeutic Innovation Center, Baylor College of Medicine. My work sits at the intersection of data science and cancer medicine—using AI and genomics to identify drug targets, understand disease mechanisms, and accelerate the path from discovery to therapy.

Before Baylor, I trained as a postdoctoral fellow with Fabian Theis at the Helmholtz Research Center in Munich, where I helped develop some of the first deep learning approaches for single-cell RNA sequencing. That work seeded a broader interest in building computational tools that the biology community can actually use.

I hold a Ph.D. in Bioinformatics from Baylor College of Medicine. I also teach a graduate-level deep learning course at the University of St. Thomas.

research
01
RNA Splicing & Cancer Therapeutics
Investigating how tumors driven by the MYC oncogene become selectively vulnerable to spliceosome-targeted therapies—work that contributed to a clinical-stage drug discovery program and a biotech spinoff.
02
Machine Learning for Single-Cell Genomics
Designing deep learning methods—from denoising autoencoders to triplet neural networks—to make sense of the high-dimensional, noisy data that single-cell sequencing produces.
03
Biomarker Discovery in Oncology
Integrating patient genomic, transcriptomic, and functional screening data to find molecular markers that predict cancer cell vulnerabilities and guide drug development decisions.
04
Translational Multi-omic Analysis
Collaborating with wet-lab and clinical teams to translate large-scale molecular data—from lung cell atlases to platelet biology—into biological insight and therapeutic hypotheses.
